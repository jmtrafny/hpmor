# The Mirror Shows Arthur
## Scene Development / Brainstorm Document

---

## The HPMOR Connection

The Mirror's inscription: "noitilov detalo partxe tnere hoc ruoy tu becafruoy ton wo hsi"

Reversed: **"I show not your face but your coherent extrapolated volition."**

This is Yudkowsky's AI alignment concept—the Mirror doesn't show what you *want*, it shows what you *would* want if you were wiser, knew more, had thought longer, resolved your internal contradictions.

---

## What Arthur Would See

### The Obvious (Wrong) Answer
His surface desire: Unified Britain. Round Table triumphant. Magic integrated for the common good. Rome rebuilt, but better.

### What the Mirror Actually Shows
**Himself, choosing to maintain the separation.**

- Himself, older, speaking to knights about why magic is not discussed
- Himself, killing a proposal he believes in
- Himself, trusting without understanding
- Himself, *becoming Merlin*

The image looks like failure. Like compromise. Like everything he's fighting against.

### The Devastating Implication
If Arthur is truly rational, the Mirror's vision should *update* him. His own CEV is telling him something.

The question: **Does he trust himself, extrapolated?**

---

## What Led To This Moment?

### The Breaking Points

**Arthur's limits exposed:**
- The plague revealed he can't save everyone through systems alone
- The Saxon wizard showed him problems that resist rational solutions
- The Round Table proposal forced him to use authority over argument
- Each crisis has cost him a piece of his idealism

**Merlin's limits exposed:**
- Arthur's persistent questioning has worn down his isolation
- The near-disasters have shown Merlin he can't prevent everything alone
- Arthur's *competence* is threatening—he's getting too close to the truth
- Merlin is tired. Centuries tired. The loneliness is crushing him.

**The mutual recognition:**
- Arthur has proven he can make terrible choices when necessary
- Merlin has seen Arthur sacrifice his principles for the greater good
- They've both watched each other suffer and hold the line
- Neither has broken the other's trust, even when it would be easier

## Why Merlin Shows Arthur the Mirror NOW

### Option A: Desperation
Something has gone wrong. Arthur is about to do something that will unravel everything—maybe restart the magic systematization project, maybe confront Merlin publicly. Merlin has no arguments left. Only the truth.

### Option B: Trust Earned
Arthur has passed enough tests. The plague. The Saxon. The Round Table vote. Merlin finally believes Arthur can handle *seeing* without needing to *understand*. The Mirror is the final test—if Arthur can accept his own CEV without explanation, he's ready.

### Option C: Merlin's Breaking Point
Merlin can't carry it alone anymore. Not won't—*can't*. The weight is destroying him. He doesn't show Arthur the Mirror to convince him. He shows Arthur because he needs someone else to *know*, even if they don't fully understand. Selfish, human, desperate.

### Option D: The Mirror Chooses
The Mirror—still carrying the Benefactor's morality—has agency. It *wants* to be seen by someone who might understand. It's been waiting for the right person. Merlin is just the conduit. The Mirror draws them both.

**My instinct: Combination of B and C.** Trust earned, but also Merlin reaching his limit. Makes both characters sympathetic.

## The Approach / Lead-Up

### Setting
- Night. Private. Somewhere neither of them has power—not Arthur's hall, not Merlin's tower.
- Perhaps the barrow where Merlin first found the Mirror?
- Or a neutral place that becomes sacred through this moment?

### The Conversation Before

**What breaks the dam?**

Maybe Arthur finally asks the right question. Not "what are you hiding?" but something that shows he's *already* figured out the shape of the secret:

> "You're not hoarding power. You're preventing something. Something that already happened."

And Merlin realizes: Arthur has reasoned his way to the edge. He's going to keep pulling at threads. The only choices are: let him unravel it wrong, or show him the truth.

**Or:**

Arthur has made a decision Merlin didn't expect—sacrificed something precious, protected something he didn't have to protect. And Merlin recognizes himself in the choice:

> "You could have won. You chose not to."
> "Yes."
> "Do you know why?"
> "No. I just knew it was wrong."

And Merlin thinks: *He's already doing it. Without knowing. Without the Mirror. What would he become if he knew?*

## The Mirror Scene Itself

### Logistics
- Is the Mirror portable? Or do they go to it?
- Does Merlin prepare Arthur at all? Or just show him?
- What does the Mirror look like physically? (HPMOR describes it, we should be consistent)

### The Moment of Looking

**Does Arthur see his CEV immediately?**

Or does he first see surface desires—the unified kingdom, the thriving systems—and then watch them *shift* as the Mirror reads deeper?

The gradual revelation might be more powerful:
1. First: The obvious dream. Arthur smiles.
2. Then: The dream flickers. Something's wrong.
3. Then: The dream *inverts*. He sees himself refusing. Limiting. Keeping secrets.
4. Then: He sees himself *at peace* with this. Content. Fulfilled.
5. The horror: This is what his wisest self wants. And he doesn't understand why.

### Arthur's Reaction

**Option A: Rejection**
> "This is wrong. This isn't me. This is what you want me to become."

He thinks Merlin is manipulating him. The trust fractures. Merlin has to talk him back.

**Option B: Confusion**
> "I don't understand. Why would I want this?"

Honest, vulnerable. Opens the door for Merlin to explain—not everything, but enough.

**Option C: Recognition**
Something in him *knows*. Maybe the same instinct that made him kill the Round Table proposal. He doesn't understand, but he recognizes the rightness of it.

> "...I've already started, haven't I? The proposal. The silence. I've already become this."

**Option D: The Rationalist Response**
> "If that's what I would want, knowing everything... then I don't know everything. What am I missing?"

He *uses* the vision as evidence. Updates. Asks the right question.

**My instinct: D, shading into C.** Arthur treats the Mirror as data, but something deeper in him resonates. Both rational and intuitive.

## What Merlin Reveals After

The Mirror doesn't explain itself. Arthur will demand answers.

**How much does Merlin tell?**

| Level | What Arthur Learns |
|-------|-------------------|
| Minimum | "There are reasons. I cannot explain them. The Mirror shows you that your wisest self accepts this." |
| Partial | "A civilization destroyed itself through good intentions. I have seen it. Integration of magic leads there." |
| Substantial | Atlantis. The Benefactor. The cascade. The erasure. The Interdict as scar tissue. |
| Full | Everything Merlin knows. Arthur becomes a true keeper. |

**The right level feels like "Partial-to-Substantial."** Enough for Arthur to understand *why*, not enough for him to reconstruct *how*. He knows the shape of the danger, not the details that could recreate it.

## The Key Emotional Beats

1. **Arthur's arrival at the question** — He's not passive. He's *earned* this through reasoning.

2. **Merlin's choice to show** — Weighted. Costly. We should feel what it takes for him to share this.

3. **The vision itself** — Strange, unsettling, beautiful. Arthur seeing himself-but-not-himself.

4. **The moment of choice** — Arthur must decide: reject the vision, or accept that his wisest self knows something he doesn't.

5. **The partial telling** — Merlin finally speaks. Not everything. But *enough*.

6. **The new relationship** — They leave this scene changed. The loneliness is broken. The weight is shared.
