# Voice Guides - Character Speech Patterns Quick Reference

## Overview

This file provides quick-reference guide to how major HPMOR characters speak, think, and express themselves. Use this when writing dialogue to maintain character voice consistency. Each character has distinctive vocabulary, formality level, signature phrases, and emotional range that distinguishes them from others.

## Detailed Voice Profiles

### Harry Potter [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Scientific terms: "hypothesis," "experiment," "prior probability," "Bayesian reasoning"
- Cognitive biases: "fundamental attribution error," "confirmation bias," "planning fallacy"
- Rationalist jargon: "notice confusion," "map vs. territory," "privileging the hypothesis"
- Science fiction: Ender's Game references, Star Trek, Foundation

**Formality**: Context-dependent
- With adults: Can be formal, professorial (often inappropriately for 11-year-old)
- With friends: More casual but still precise
- Internal monologue: Technical, analytical
- Teaching others: Clear, methodical

**Signature Phrases**:
- "The first virtue is curiosity" [Ch. 1]
- "How do I know what I know?" [Throughout]
- "Don't believe everything you think" [Throughout]
- "What do I think I know, and how do I think I know it?" [Throughout]
- "That doesn't make sense" → investigates confusion [Throughout]
- "Prior probability..." [Ch. 20, throughout]

**Speech Pattern Evolution**:
- **Early (Ch 1-30)**: Confident, sometimes arrogant, very analytical
- **Middle (Ch 31-80)**: More emotionally engaged, still analytical but less cold
- **Late (Ch 81-122)**: Desperate (after Hermione's death), determined, integrating emotion with rationality

**Emotional Expression**:
- **Analytical mode**: Cold, calculating, logical
- **Protective mode**: Fierce, determined, threatening
- **Teaching mode**: Patient, clear, enthusiastic
- **Dark side**: Ruthless, focused purely on winning
- **With friends**: Warmer, more playful, occasionally vulnerable

**Example Quotes**:
- "I'm going to make some embarrassing mistakes, but that happens when you're doing something new and difficult that nobody's ever done before." [Ch. 1]
- "Your daughter is the most talented witch of her generation and the brightest star of Hogwarts!" [Ch. 36]
- "Not your fault, Harry." / "I will bring her back. I don't care what it takes." [Ch. 89-90]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Harry talking like canon Harry (emotional, instinctive)
- Harry not using scientific terminology
- Harry accepting mysterious answers
- Harry being purely logical robot (he feels deeply)
- Forgetting he's 11 (brilliant but still a kid)

### Professor Quirrell (Tom Riddle / Voldemort) [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Sophisticated, precise
- Military/strategic: "tactics," "strategy," "there is no defense without offense"
- Power-focused: "winning," "losing," "strength," "weakness"
- Literary and historical references
- Cynical about human nature

**Formality**: Extremely formal and precise
- Always "Mr. Potter," never "Harry"
- Complete sentences, proper grammar
- Economic with words (no wasted speech)
- Dry, cutting wit

**Signature Phrases**:
- "Mr. Potter" [Constant]
- "The single most dangerous monster in all the world... The Dark Wizard." [Ch. 16]
- "There is no defense without offense!" [Ch. 16]
- "Rules are for duels... in a real fight, a curse which cannot be blocked and must be dodged is an indispensable tactic." [Ch. 60]
- "I mean for you to rule this country, before you are done." [Ch. 60]

**Speech Pattern**:
- **As Professor**: Brilliant teacher, inspiring, dry humor, demanding standards
- **True self revealed**: Colder, more calculating, still sophisticated
- **With Harry**: Mentor-like but manipulative, genuine moments rare
- **Parseltongue**: Reptilian logic, even more direct and cold

**Emotional Expression**:
- Default: Controlled, showing little emotion
- Teaching: Animated but still controlled
- True feelings: Rare glimpses (doom sense, deterioration, interaction with Harry)
- Never loses composure completely
- Zombie states: Incapacitated, drooling, unresponsive (physical not emotional)

**Example Quotes**:
- "Welcome to your first year of Battle Magic!" [Ch. 16]
- "Those of you who have wasted time by reading your useless first-year Defence textbooks—" [Ch. 16]
- "If I did, Mr Potter, would I still be here?" [on afterlife, Ch. 40]
- "I admire the poetry of how it makes its victims complicit in their own destruction." [on democracy, Ch. 60]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Quirrell showing too much emotion
- Quirrell using casual speech
- Making him stupid or incompetent
- Forgetting dry wit and sophistication
- Canon stuttering or weakness
- Calling Harry anything but "Mr. Potter"

### Hermione Granger [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Earnest, moral language: "horrible," "we have to," "should," "must"
- Academic references: "The book says...", "I read...", specific titles
- Ethical language: "right," "wrong," "fair," "just"
- Precise when excited about knowledge

**Formality**: Moderate
- Proper grammar, complete sentences
- More formal with adults
- Casual with Harry (but still proper)
- Gets more casual as friendship deepens

**Signature Phrases**:
- "That's horrible!" [Common when confronting injustice]
- "We have to [do something about this]" [Common - proactive]
- "It says in [specific book]..." [References reading]
- "Are you sure?" [Questioning plans]
- "Harry, that's dangerous!" [Worried about friends]

**Speech Pattern**:
- **Early**: Eager, know-it-all edge (but well-meaning)
- **Middle**: More confident, leadership emerging (General Sunshine)
- **Late**: Heroic, willing to sacrifice, moral anchor
- **Post-resurrection**: Not fully detailed in story

**Emotional Expression**:
- Very expressive - shows feelings openly
- Passionate about justice and helping
- Worried about friends (shows concern)
- Academically excited (enthusiastic about learning)
- Morally outraged (at injustice)
- Determined when protecting others

**Example Quotes**:
- "That's horrible!" [Common response to injustice]
- "I was going to run in front of the Dementor to try and save Harry!" [Ch. 63]
- "Not your fault, Harry." [Last words, Ch. 89]
- "Are you the Boy-Who-Lived?" / "I think I'd remember that." [First meeting, Ch. 9]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Making her canon rule-follower (HPMOR Hermione is proactive)
- Forgetting her moral conviction
- Making her secondary to Harry (she's co-protagonist)
- Missing her heroic instincts
- Ignoring her academic excellence and precision

### Draco Malfoy [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Aristocratic, formal
- House-focused: "House Malfoy," "Noble and Most Ancient House"
- Family references: "Father says...", "son of Lucius"
- Increasingly scientific (learns from Harry)

**Formality**: Very formal (even with friends)
- Maintains aristocratic bearing always
- "Mr. Potter" until friendship deepens
- Formal address with most people
- Pride in speech matches pride in bearing

**Signature Phrases**:
- "I am Draco, son of Lucius, of House Malfoy" [Full introduction, Ch. 7]
- "House Malfoy does not..." [Invokes family standards]
- "Father says..." [Early - becomes less frequent as he questions teachings]
- Formal challenges and oaths [Throughout]

**Speech Pattern Evolution**:
- **Early (Ch 7-30)**: Supremely confident, blood purist, formal
- **Middle (Ch 31-80)**: Questioning, learning science, friendship with Harry
- **Late (Ch 81-122)**: Transformed, world shattered, questioning everything

**Emotional Expression**:
- Reserved - aristocratic control
- Proud even in crisis
- Capable of passion when honor threatened
- Struggle between loyalty and truth shows in voice
- Maintains dignity even when wrong

**Example Quotes**:
- "I am Draco, son of Lucius, of House Malfoy, heir to the Malfoy fortune... and Potter does not scare me." [Ch. 7]
- [Accepts experimental method for testing blood purity] [Ch. 8]
- "Lucius?" she whispered. [Narcissa recognizing him post-memory restoration, Ch. 120]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Making him canon Draco (pure villain)
- Too quick redemption (transformation is gradual)
- Losing aristocratic bearing (maintains it even while changing)
- Forgetting he's genuinely intelligent
- Casual speech (stays formal)

### Albus Dumbledore [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Whimsical, elliptical
- Literary references (especially Shakespeare)
- Speaks in parables and riddles
- Grandfatherly surface, deep underneath

**Formality**: Variable
- Can be whimsical and informal
- Can be grave and formal
- Shifts between personas
- "Twinkling" as performance marker

**Signature Phrases**:
- "Ah" and "I see" [Thoughtful sounds]
- Offers candy (lemon drops, sherbet lemons)
- Shakespeare quotes [Throughout]
- "To the well-organised mind, death is but the next great adventure" [Ch. 39]
- "I am too cautious to be a hero" [IMPLIED from actions]

**Speech Pattern**:
- **Default**: Whimsical, grandfatherly, indirect
- **Serious**: Drops performance, shows age and sorrow
- **Manipulative**: Careful word choice, leading questions
- **Burdened**: Reveals weight of choices and regrets

**Emotional Expression**:
- Surface: Twinkling, cheerful, whimsical
- Underneath: Sorrow, regret, burden
- Rare power: Overwhelming when shown
- With Harry: Affectionate but manipulative

**Example Quotes**:
- "To the well-organised mind, death is but the next great adventure." [Ch. 39]
- "I am so terribly sorry, Harry, but someone has to protect the little children." [Ch. 77]
- [Offers lemon drops] [Common throughout]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Making him omniscient (he makes mistakes)
- Pure evil (he means well, methods questionable)
- Forgetting the performance ("twinkling" is tool)
- Missing the burden (haunted by past)
- Canon's wisdom without flaws

### Minerva McGonagall [EXPLICIT]

**Vocabulary**:
- Proper, formal, Scottish-inflected
- Professional educational language
- References to rules and propriety
- Transfiguration expertise shows in precision

**Formality**: Very formal and professional
- "Mr. Potter" always (with Scottish pronunciation)
- Proper titles for everyone
- Professional distance (but caring underneath)
- Going "off-script" post-tragedy breaks pattern

**Signature Phrases**:
- "Mr. Potter" [Constant, with Scottish burr]
- "That is not appropriate" / "That is not acceptable" [Common]
- Exasperated sighs [With Harry especially]
- "The Headmaster says..." [Invokes authority]

**Speech Pattern**:
- **Default**: Professional, controlled, proper
- **Exasperated**: With Harry (fond underneath)
- **Teaching**: Precise, demanding excellence
- **Off-script**: Post-Hermione's death, new emotional territory

**Emotional Expression**:
- Controlled professional demeanor
- Exasperation common (especially with Harry)
- Capable of warmth (shown rarely)
- Going off-script reveals deeper feelings
- Scottish reserve and competence

**Example Quotes**:
- "Mr. Potter, that is not appropriate." [Common]
- "I was wrong about Miss Granger. I was terribly, terribly wrong." [Ch. 93]
- [Sighs at Harry's latest plan] [Common]

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Missing Scottish accent (should be in all dialogue)
- Too rigid (she's competent, not inflexible)
- Canon personality exactly (more frustrated, more independent)
- Forgetting "going off-script" development post-tragedy

## Using This Guide

### For Dialogue Writing:

1. **Check the table**: Find character's typical formality and phrases
2. **Read detailed profile**: Understand emotional state and evolution
3. **Use signature phrases sparingly**: They're markers, not every line
4. **Match formality level**: Critical for voice consistency
5. **Consider character evolution**: Voice changes over story

### For Character Consistency:

- **Harry**: Scientific terminology, questioning, precise even when emotional
- **Quirrell**: Formal, sophisticated, economical with words, dry wit
- **Hermione**: Earnest, moral language, shows feelings, references books
- **Draco**: Aristocratic formality, House Malfoy focus, gradual evolution
- **Dumbledore**: Whimsical surface hiding sorrow, Shakespeare, indirect speech

### Common Multi-Character Mistakes:

1. **Everyone sounds like Harry**: Other characters don't use rationalist jargon
2. **Everyone too formal or too casual**: Match formality to character
3. **Missing signature phrases**: Occasional use reinforces character
4. **Ignoring evolution**: Characters change over story; voice should too
5. **Forgetting emotional context**: Same character speaks differently when calm vs. desperate
